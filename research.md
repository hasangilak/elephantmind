# How Memory Champions Train and Compete: A Practical Research Report for Game Design

## TL;DR
- Elite memory athletes don't have special brains — they convert abstract data (numbers, cards, binary) into vivid images using a small set of techniques (the Method of Loci/memory palace plus a number/card image "system" like the Major, Dominic, PAO, Ben, or Shadow systems), then drill those systems daily on platforms like Memory League, Memocamp, and Anki. The memory palace is the universal backbone; the image system is the personal "encoding engine."
- Competition has two worlds: the traditional 10-discipline "decathlon" (WMSC and IAM, 5-to-60-minute events over 3 days) and the fast, online head-to-head Memory League (5 disciplines, ≤60-second memorization). A training game should mirror Memory League's bite-sized, gamified format while teaching the underlying systems.
- Concrete benchmarks to design around: a deck of 52 cards memorized in ~12–13 seconds (world record), 80 digits in ~11 seconds, 30 images in ~8 seconds, and ~3,000–3,400 digits in one hour. Beginners reach "competition-ready" in 10–20 hours; elite level takes a few years of ~30–60 min/day.

## Key Findings

1. **The memory palace (Method of Loci) is the universal technique.** Across surveyed top-50 world championship athletes, essentially all cite the method of loci as their most important strategy. It works by placing vivid images at known spatial locations and "walking" the route to recall them in order.

2. **Numbers and cards require a pre-built image "system."** Unlike words (which can be improvised), digits and cards must be converted via a memorized lookup table. The progression of systems by sophistication is: number-shape/rhyme (1 image/digit) → Major System & Dominic System (1 image per 2 digits, 100 images) → PAO (encodes 6 digits or 3 cards per image) → 3-digit systems and 2-card systems like the Ben System (2,704 images) and Shadow System (1,352 images).

3. **Training is daily, deliberate, and modest in duration.** Alex Mullen (3× world champion) trains about 30 minutes a day — in his own words, "I spend about 25% of that time on speed drills and the other 75% practicing competition events." Beginners can become competition-ready in 10–20 hours.

4. **The competition disciplines split into three formats** — WMSC (10 disciplines), IAM (similar, with concrete "random images" instead of abstract spots, and shorter binary), and Memory League (5 short, head-to-head digital disciplines). Each tests different sub-skills.

5. **World records reveal the performance ceiling** to design difficulty curves around: 52 cards in 12.74s (IAM) / 13.96s (WMSC); 3,412 digits in one hour; 30 Memory League images in 7.69s.

6. **Plateaus and characteristic errors are well-documented** — "ghosting" (old images bleeding through reused palaces), image "border-blur" from similar images, and order-swapping within a locus. Champions overcome these with palace rotation, "spring cleaning," speed-drilling above comfort level, and error-correction practice.

## Details

### PART 1 — HOW MEMORY CHAMPIONS TRAIN

#### The Core Techniques

**Method of Loci / Memory Palace.** The foundational technique, traced to the Greek poet Simonides of Ceos (5th c. BC) and described in the Roman *Ad Herennium*. The athlete picks a familiar location (home, school, route), defines an ordered set of "loci" (distinct spots), and places vivid mental images at each. Recall = mentally re-walking the route. A 2017 Stanford/Donders study in *Neuron* (Dresler et al., March 8, 2017) trained 51 mnemonics-naïve participants with a six-week course of daily online sessions in the method of loci (40 half-hour sessions); the trained group improved from recalling 26 words to 62 of 72 words — approaching the 71/72 that top-50 memory athletes achieved — and their brain functional-connectivity patterns shifted to resemble athletes', with effects persisting at four months. The research is unambiguous that memory athletes "do not seem to be associated with extraordinary brain anatomy or general cognitive superiority" — the skill is trained. Alex Mullen uses about 3,000 loci across roughly 80 memory palaces, typically 5 loci per room.

**The Major System (phonetic number system).** Converts each digit 0–9 into a consonant sound (0=s/z, 1=t/d, 2=n, 3=m, 4=r, 5=l, 6=j/ch/sh, 7=k/g, 8=f/v, 9=p/b), then builds words by adding vowels. E.g., 32 → M,N → "moon." It gives a fixed image for each 00–99. Historical roots run through Pierre Hérigone and Stanislaus Mink von Wennsshein; it is named after Major Beniowski (1845). It is the base layer for most advanced systems.

**The Dominic System.** Invented by Dominic O'Brien (8× world champion). Maps digits to letters (1=A, 2=B, 3=C, 4=D, 5=E, 6=S, 7=G, 8=H, 9=N, 0=O); each 2-digit number becomes a person's initials with a characteristic action (e.g., 15=AE=Albert Einstein; 1516 = Einstein performing the action linked to 16). It is a Person-Action system; combined with palaces it becomes "Hotel Dominic." O'Brien was inspired in 1987 watching Creighton Carvello memorize a deck on the BBC's *Record Breakers*; he holds a Guinness record (May 1, 2002) for 2,808 cards (54 decks) after a single viewing, with only 8 errors (four self-corrected).

**PAO (Person-Action-Object).** Each 2-digit number (00–99) gets a unique Person, Action, and Object. Six digits collapse into one composite image (Person of digits 1–2 + Action of 3–4 + Object of 5–6). For cards, every card gets a PAO so 3 cards = one image. Used by virtually every competitor at some stage; both Mullen and Pridmore started with PAO before moving to denser systems.

**The Ben System.** Created by Ben Pridmore (3× world champion). A consonant-vowel-consonant scheme giving a single image for every 2-card combination (2,704 images), every 3-digit group (000–999), or every 10 binary digits. Pridmore places three "objects" per locus, encoding 2 cards/image and 6 cards/locus. Example: ace of hearts + two of clubs = "fan"; famously 4♥+10♠ = an opossum and K♣+9♠ = a Transformers toy.

**The Shadow System.** Created by Lance Tschirhart and also used by Alex Mullen. A 2-card system needing only 1,352 images (vs. Ben's 2,704) by using a "shadow" image for the reversed card pair, built on the Major System with three consonants per card pair. Mullen's variant overlaps 992 images with his number system, so he needs only ~360 card-specific images. In Mullen's own account: "I created my 1352-image cards system in June of 2014. It took until approximately October (training about 30 minutes each day) until I had reached... equaling my personal bests set using my old PAO system."

**The "Million Digit" / Pi challenge approach.** For very large fixed targets (such as pi), athletes use long permanent palaces and Dominic O'Brien's "Rule of Five" spaced review. Nelson Dellis recites pi to 10,000 digits; to claim a pi record you must recite 50 randomly chosen 5-digit chunks plus surrounding digits while timed. Pridmore has memorized roughly 50,000 digits of pi. The Guinness pi record is 70,000 decimal places, set by Rajveer Meena (India) at VIT University, Vellore, on March 21, 2015, recited blindfolded in 9 hours 27 minutes.

**Link/Story method and Peg systems.** The Link/Story method chains images into a narrative (good for words, weaker for ordered precision). Peg systems attach items to fixed "pegs" (number-rhyme: 1=bun, 2=shoe; number-shape; or Major-System pegs). These are the entry-level techniques beginners learn first.

#### How Different Information Types Are Encoded

- **Numbers:** Major/Dominic/PAO/3-digit systems → images → palace. Top competitors place 2 three-digit images per locus (6 digits/locus).
- **Binary digits:** Converted to decimal in chunks then encoded. With a 2-digit system + 3 images/locus you get 18 binary digits/locus; the Ben System encodes 30 binary digits per locus — exactly one competition row. Conversion path: binary groups → decimal → existing number image.
- **Playing cards:** 1-card systems (52 images), PAO (3 cards/image), or 2-card systems (Ben/Shadow). Mullen converts each consecutive 2-card pair into one image, 2 images per locus.
- **Names & faces:** Largely association-based, NOT loci. Convert the name to a sound-alike image (e.g., "chervil" → "sure-villain"), then anchor it to a distinctive facial feature, or "give a face a place" (O'Brien). Notably, Katie Kermode (multiple-record holder) says for names "there's literally no technique that I use" — pure repetition/association.
- **Random words:** Improvised images placed in a palace, usually 2 words/locus.
- **Abstract images:** WMSC uses black-and-white random spots; athletes assign preset images to recurring shapes/textures (Mullen created preset images for ~150 background textures). Difficulty: the images resist verbalization.
- **Historic/future dates:** Fictional event + 4-digit year (1000–2099). Convert year to a number image, link it to the event. Among the fastest disciplines because there is no long sequential ordering.
- **Spoken numbers:** Digits read aloud at 1/second; no chance to review, so encoding must keep pace in real time. One error ends the score — the highest-pressure discipline.

#### Training Routines, Tools & Drills

- **Volume:** Mullen ~30 min/day (he told MWB: "between 30 minutes to an hour a day, usually. You do that over three years and you get pretty good... I don't think I have a very special memory. I forget where I put things all the time"). Enrico Marraffa (2024 IAM champion) trained roughly 3 hours/day for five months before winning. Pridmore spent about 9 hours/week memorizing decks in his early years. Casual competition-readiness: ~10–20 hours over a few weeks.
- **Building & reusing palaces:** Champions maintain large stocks (Mullen ~80 palaces / 3,000 loci; Dellis cycles palaces so they "empty out," keeping fresh ones for competition).
- **Speed training:** Push memorization faster than comfortable to break plateaus; a popular tool is the AutoHotkey "digital metronome" that forces item advancement at fixed intervals on Memory League.
- **Spaced repetition / system drilling:** Athletes learn their 100 / 1,000 / 2,704 images using Anki flashcards (Mullen keeps a dedicated "Memory Sports" Anki deck). O'Brien's "Rule of Five" review schedule: immediately, 24h, 1 week, 1 month, 3 months.
- **Error-correction practice:** Reviewing failed attempts, identifying weak images, and applying "directionality" rules to prevent order-swaps (making the first image larger, or having it act upon the second).

**Software/platforms that already exist (directly relevant to game design):**
- **Memory League** (memoryleague.com) — the dominant online training + head-to-head competition app; founded as the Extreme Memory Tournament by Nelson Dellis & Simon Orton. 5 disciplines, level-based, leaderboards, daily challenge.
- **Memocamp** — full traditional-discipline practice with leaderboards (Mullen's early go-to; used in the Donders/Dresler study).
- **Art of Memory** trainer (mt.artofmemory.com/training) and the Art of Memory forum/wiki.
- **standard-memory.com** and the **IAM Training Software** — for 40-digit-row competition-format practice.
- **Anki** — spaced repetition for learning image systems.
- **Memory Athlete** (iOS app), plus various brain-training apps.

#### Beginners vs. Elite
Beginners start with a single small palace (10–25 loci), the Link/Story method for words, and a basic 2-digit or 1-card system (buildable in under an hour). Elites maintain dozens of palaces, dense 2-card/3-digit systems, drill speed daily, and train "close to real tournament conditions" leading up to events (Simon Reinhard). The jump from PAO to a 2-card system took Mullen about four months of daily practice just to match his old personal bests.

#### Common Mistakes, Plateaus & Cognitive Challenges
- **"Ghosting"** — images from previous uses of a palace bleed into new attempts. Solution: rotate palaces; Foer notes athletes do a "spring cleaning," mentally emptying palaces before competitions.
- **"Border-blur"** — too-similar images get confused.
- **Order-swapping** within a locus (which of two images came first). Solution: directionality rules.
- **Plateaus** — Reinhard: "Sometimes you hit a plateau for quite some time but once you manage to crack a certain threshold, it gets so much easier." Speed-drilling above comfort breaks through.
- **Pressure/nerves** — even elites struggle; one error voids a whole row or card trial. Mullen credits the techniques with building the concentration to "sit for three whole days" memorizing.
- **Reading bottleneck** — research found the *reading*/recognition step (not the association step) is the main speed bottleneck in rapid disciplines.

#### Champion Insights & Quotes
- **Nelson Dellis** (5–6× USA champion): "The memory palace... is the bread and butter of what memory athletes will use." He got into the sport after his grandmother's Alzheimer's and insists, "I was a good student, but my memory was average."
- **Alex Mullen** (3× world champion): "I don't think I have a very special memory. I forget where I put things all the time." Trains ~30 min/day; became world champion within ~2 years.
- **Dominic O'Brien**: dyslexic and "painfully slow" per school reports; emphasizes imagination + association + location and the Rule of Five.
- **Ben Pridmore**: "Anyone could do it." Associates each of 2,704 two-card combinations with an image.
- **Joshua Foer** (2006 USA champion, *Moonwalking with Einstein*): "all mnemonic techniques are essentially based on the concept of elaborative encoding... the more meaningful something is, the easier it is to remember." His book inspired Mullen and many others.
- **Boris Konrad** (neuroscientist & athlete): co-authored the *Neuron* study proving the techniques bulk up memory networks.
- **Katie Kermode**: relies on no technique for names; holds multiple word/names records.

### PART 2 — THE COMPETITION DISCIPLINES

#### The Three Formats
1. **WMSC (World Memory Sports Council)** — original body (Tony Buzan & Raymond Keene, 1991). 10 disciplines over 3 days at the World Championship; in-person, paper-based verification; maximum ~10,000 points total. Uses 15-min abstract images (black-and-white random spots).
2. **IAM (International Association of Memory)** — split off in 2016/2017. Similar disciplines but uses concrete "random images" (5-min) instead of abstract spots, and shorter binary; permits hybrid/digital recall. Current IAM world champion: Vishvaa Rajakumar (India), 2025.
3. **Memory League** — fully online, head-to-head, 5 short disciplines (≤60s memorization). Knockout/league format with a "World Tour" (3 seasons + 3 Slams) and a double-elimination World Championship of the top 16.

Both traditional bodies scale events: **national** (e.g., 5-min numbers, 15-min numbers), **international** (30-min versions), and **world** (60-min versions) standards. Recall time is typically 2–3× the memorization time. There are two attempts on 5-min numbers and speed cards, and three trials of varying length for spoken numbers.

#### The 10 Traditional Disciplines (rules, times, scoring)
1. **Speed Cards** — memorize 1 shuffled 52-card deck as fast as possible (5-min max). Always the final discipline. Scored on time; one error = zero for the trial (must be error-free to count). Two trials, best counts.
2. **Random (Hour) Cards** — most decks in 1 hour (national/international versions shorter, e.g., 10-min, 30-min). Cards in sequence; counts until first error; row/deck scoring.
3. **Speed Numbers** — most digits in 5 minutes, rows of 40 digits. One error in a row = half points (20); two+ errors = zero for that row. Two attempts.
4. **15/30/60-min Numbers (Marathon/Hour Numbers)** — endurance digit memorization, same row scoring.
5. **Binary Digits** — rows of 30 binary digits; 5-min (national) or 30-min (international). Same half/zero row scoring.
6. **Names & Faces** — memorize names paired with photos (15-min standard); recall the name under each reordered face. One point per correct first/last name.
7. **Abstract Images** (WMSC) — rows of 5 black-and-white random spot-images (15-min); recall the order within each row; 5 points/correct row, −1 for an error.
8. **Historic/Future Dates** — ~100+ fictional events with years 1000–2099 (5-min); recall the year for each scrambled event. One point per correct date, −0.5 per wrong.
9. **Random Words** — columns of 20 words (15-min); recall in order. Errors cost points per column.
10. **Spoken Numbers** — digits read aloud at 1/second (up to ~30 min); recall in order; one error ends the count. Three trials of increasing length.

#### Memory League (5 disciplines, head-to-head)
Standard solo levels and head-to-head matches; memorization ≤60s, with the goal of memorizing a *fixed* quantity as fast as possible (the inverse of the classical "max in fixed time"):
- **Cards** — 52 cards.
- **Numbers** — 80 digits.
- **Images** — 30 images in order.
- **Words** — 50 words in order.
- **Names** — 30 names/faces (first names only; an "International Names" variant exists).
Matches are best-of games; the World Tour uses divisions of 8 with 6-game matches plus Slam knockout tournaments.

The **USA Memory Championship** uses Memory League for online qualifiers (Cards 52 in 300s; Images 30; Names 30; Numbers 80; Words 50) and unique on-stage finals events (Tea Party, Three Strikes/Words, Double Deck O'Cards).

#### What Each Discipline Tests
- **Speed Cards:** pure encoding + placement speed; the "blue riband" event. Tony Buzan likened breaking the 30-second barrier to the 4-minute mile.
- **Spoken Numbers:** real-time encoding with zero review; concentration under no-error pressure.
- **Hour disciplines:** endurance, palace management, sustained focus.
- **Binary:** efficient high-density encoding.
- **Names & Faces:** association + face-feature linking; least dependent on palaces.
- **Abstract Images:** visual encoding of non-verbalizable shapes.
- **Historic Dates:** rapid associative pairing (the fastest 5-min discipline).
- **Words:** improvisational image creation.

#### Competition Structure
- **National championships** (USA, Canadian, Indian, German, Mongolian, etc.) feed into international/world events.
- **World Memory Championship** — 3 days, 10 disciplines, run separately by WMSC and IAM since 2017.
- **Memory League World Championship / World Tour** — online, seasonal, head-to-head, top-16 double-elimination.
- **Other formats:** Memoriad (includes mental calculation), MAA Memo Games.
- **Titles:** WMSC awards Grand Master of Memory (International Grandmaster is the highest); roughly 200 grandmasters exist.
- Mongolia, China, and increasingly India dominate current rankings; recent champions include Naranzul Otgon-Ulaan (WMSC) and Vishvaa Rajakumar (IAM/Memory League 2025).

## Recommendations (for building a memory-training game)

**Stage 1 — Onboard with the universal core.** Teach the memory palace first using a small fixed palace (10–15 loci) and the Link/Story method on random words and images — these need no pre-built system, so players get a "wow" win in minutes (mirror Memory League's Images discipline: 6 images in groups of 2 at level 1, scaling up). Benchmark: take a beginner from ~5 items to ~20 items in the first session — the same magnitude of jump the *Neuron* study produced (26 → 62 words in six weeks).

**Stage 2 — Introduce a number system as the core progression mechanic.** Gate the Major System (00–99, 100 images) behind early levels and use Anki-style spaced repetition to drill it — this is the game's central "leveling" engine. Then unlock cards (reuse 52 of the number images). Provide a 2-digit → PAO → 3-digit/2-card upgrade tree so advancement maps to real expertise.

**Stage 3 — Adopt Memory League's format as the competitive layer.** Its 5 disciplines (Cards, Numbers, Images, Words, Names), with ≤60s memorization and "memorize a fixed amount as fast as possible" scoring, are ideal for short, replayable, head-to-head rounds and leaderboards. Add a "speed metronome" mode that forces faster encoding to break plateaus.

**Difficulty curve / benchmarks to target:**
- Beginner: 20 words, 30–60 digits, a partial deck in 5 minutes.
- Intermediate: full deck under 2 minutes; 100+ digits in 5 min.
- Advanced: deck under 30s; 300+ digits in 5 min.
- Elite (world-record reference): deck in ~12–13s; 80 digits in ~11s; 30 images in ~8s; 3,000+ digits/hour.

**Build in anti-plateau and error-correction features:** palace rotation/"spring cleaning" mechanics to fight ghosting; directionality hints to reduce order-swaps; replay of failed attempts highlighting weak images; spaced-repetition reminders following the Rule of Five (immediate, 24h, 1 week, 1 month, 3 months).

**Teach via champions' framing:** lean on the repeated, evidence-backed message that this is trainable, not innate ("I had an average memory") — it lowers the barrier and is precisely what the *Neuron* research shows.

**Thresholds that change the design:** If players stall at system memorization (the Major/PAO tables), add more gamified spaced repetition before introducing speed. If retention of the palace itself is the bottleneck, supply pre-built palaces (as Memory League effectively does) rather than forcing players to invent them.

## Caveats
- **Records shift fast and the two bodies keep separate lists.** WMSC's official statistics site was under reconstruction at the time of research, so some WMSC figures rely on a community "BETA" compilation (global-memory.org) and should be treated as approximate; IAM records are from the live iam-stats.org. Two IAM entries (5-min Images 802 and 30-min Numbers 2,132, both Enrico Marraffa) are dated "2026" in IAM's table, reflecting a competition labeled "European Championship 2026" — flagged as unusual given the current date (June 2026); they appear to be very recent legitimate records but should be re-verified.
- **Some widely repeated records are disputed or superseded** (e.g., 2017 WMSC binary/hour-number figures such as 3,979 binary and 1,600 hour numbers were later beaten; do not treat them as current).
- **"Photographic memory" is a myth** in this community — all results come from trained technique.
- **Names disciplines vary** (first-only in Memory League, first+last in classical), and abstract vs. concrete images differ by body — a game should pick one convention per mode.
- **Several quotes are drawn from interviews/secondary outlets** (CNBC, Big Think, Magnetic Memory Method, Mullen Memory, MWB) rather than primary transcripts; wording is accurate but may be lightly edited in sources.
